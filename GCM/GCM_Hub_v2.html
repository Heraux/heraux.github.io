Try It Yourself
Three things to discover in each tool
1
Open Tool 01. You'll see a 3D shape floating in the dark — two glowing rings connected by lines.
Click the button at the bottom that says ① Circle → Circle. The shape looks like a smooth tube.
Now click ② Circle → Star.

Write what you see change. Do the connecting lines look smooth or twisted? Does anything look stressed?
2
Look at the top-left corner of the tool. There's a number that starts with "boundary mismatch".
Click through presets ①, ②, ③, ④ one at a time. Watch that number.

Write the number you see at each preset. Does it go up, down, or jump around?
3
Click preset ⑤ Collapse.
The connecting lines disappear — the surface falls apart.
But look carefully: the two glowing rings, A (blue) and B (orange), are still there.

What does it mean that A and B survive but the surface between them doesn't? Write your best guess — no wrong answer.
What you just found
The surface between A and B only exists when the two shapes are similar enough. When they're too different, it breaks. This is the same kind of thing that happens when a soap film snaps, or when a material fractures — there's a tipping point. You just found it by watching.
1
Open Tool 02. You'll see two glowing rings connected by a surface — it looks like a tube or a funnel.
Click preset ① Still at the bottom. Look at the connecting lines — they run straight up and down.
Now click ② Twist. Then ③ Stress.

Describe what happens to those lines. Do they stay straight? Which preset is where you first notice them bending?
2
Click ⊕ advanced controls — a row of sliders appears.
Find the slider labeled V — twist. Drag it slowly all the way to the right.
Watch the shape change. Watch the meter in the top-left labeled "torus blend".

At what point does the shape stop looking like a tube between two rings and start looking like a donut? Write the twist number shown on screen when that happens.
3
Drag the twist slider back to 0°. Two separate rings. No donut.
Drag it to 360°. One donut. No separate rings — they merged.

Something surprising happened: the same shape went from "two things connected" to "one closed loop." Write what you think happened to the two boundary rings — where did they go?
What you just found
You watched one shape continuously deform into a completely different one without ever breaking. That's called a homotopy in mathematics. The twist parameter is doing all the work — it's one dial that controls whether you're looking at a tube or a torus. This exact kind of smooth transition shows up in physics whenever a system changes phase.
1
Open Tool 03. On the left panel you'll see shape buttons for A, in the center for P, and on the right for B.
Set A to TET, P to OCT, and B to TET.
Look at the top-right metrics. Find SYM.

What does the symmetry score say? Now change B to CUBE instead. Write what happens to the SYM metric and how the shape looks different.
2
Open the ◈ DISCOVER panel (top center). Click ▶ AUTO SWEEP.
Watch the discovery log fill up with named configurations as it cycles through combinations automatically.

Find any entry tagged NAMED in gold. Click it to apply it. Write the name it shows and describe what the 3D shape looks like for that configuration.
3
Set A and B both to the same shape — try CUBE for both, P to PT.
Now find the Twist A slider and drag it to 45°. Then try 90°.
Use the ★ SAVE STATE button to save a configuration you find interesting.

At what twist angle does the shape look most surprising or unexpected to you? Write the angle and describe what you see.
What you just found
When A and B are the same shape, the symmetry score is PERFECT — P sits exactly between two identical boundaries. Twist breaks that symmetry in a controlled way, rotating one boundary relative to the other. The named configurations you found in the sweep are geometric structures with real mathematical identities: prisms, antiprisms, and compound polyhedra all emerge from simple A·P·B choices.
