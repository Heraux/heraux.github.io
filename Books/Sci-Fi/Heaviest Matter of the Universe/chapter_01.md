# Chapter 1

Augustus Veil set his coffee mug down on the coaster and adjusted it a quarter-inch to the left, because the coaster had drifted and a man should keep his systems current.

The coaster was 3D-printed — matte black, exactly the diameter of the mug base, with a small flag standing up from one edge that read **COFFEE GOES HERE** in eight-point font. He'd printed it eleven months ago after the fourth sticky note migrated. Sticky notes were provisional. The ring stain still on the desk to the right was archaeological evidence of the provisional era. He'd left it there on purpose. A reminder that problems got solved properly or they didn't get solved.

The coffee was terrible. Briar coffee was always terrible. Two thousand scientists staffing the most advanced research facility in human history and the third-floor machine dispensed something that tasted like hot rust filtered through institutional regret. He drank it anyway. He drank it every night. The mug was warm in his hands and the building was quiet and the hallway lights had dimmed to their overnight amber — 2700 Kelvin, he'd checked once because he was that kind of person — and Augustus Veil was alone with the best idea he'd ever had, which meant this was the best part of his day, possibly his year, and he planned to enjoy every second of it before someone needed him for something less interesting.

His office was a masterpiece of optimized chaos. Three monitors in a shallow arc, the left one permanently displaying a terminal window he hadn't closed in eleven days, the center running his derivation workspace, the right showing a live feed of the sky outside that he almost never looked at anymore. Papers stacked in columns organized by a system only he understood. Two whiteboards covered in equations, one of which contained a small drawing of a duck he didn't remember making. A framed photo of his PhD cohort, seven people smiling in a way that suggested they didn't yet know what postdoc salaries looked like.

And above it all, on the ceiling, a water stain shaped like Italy.

"Evening, Italy," VB said. "We're going to have a good night."

Italy said nothing. Italy never said anything. But VB had been talking to that stain for three years now, and their relationship was the most stable one in his life, so he saw no reason to stop.

He set the coffee down on the coaster — center, where it belonged — and pulled up the file. It lived on an encrypted partition in a folder labeled BUDGET PROJECTIONS FY27, because if there was one thing guaranteed to make every human being on Earth lose interest in a folder, it was the word "budget" followed by a fiscal year. Inside that folder, nested three directories deep behind names like OVERHEAD ESTIMATES and CAFETERIA VENDOR REVIEW, was a file called wr_main.tex.

Wiggle Room.

He hadn't told anyone about it. Not Simon, who would have immediately seen the implications and then spent three hours telling VB everything he'd gotten wrong — fair, Simon was occasionally right — but this wasn't ready for that yet. Not his section lead. Not anyone on the HTM team. The idea was still too fragile, too audacious, too likely to get him laughed out of a room he very much needed to be taken seriously in. Especially tomorrow.

Tomorrow. He let the thought pass through without grabbing it. Tomorrow was tomorrow. Right now was the math, and the math was being cooperative for once, and VB had learned long ago that when the math decided to cooperate you didn't waste time thinking about meetings.

He cracked his knuckles — a habit Simon called "acoustically criminal" — and reached for the second drawer from the left. Not the first. The second. He didn't think about it. His hand went there the way hands go places they've been a thousand times, and he pulled out a specific pen — the good one, the one with the right weight — uncapped it, set it to the right of the keyboard where it would be reachable without looking, and opened the derivation where he'd left it last night.

---

The core of Wiggle Room was deceptively simple, in the way that all ideas that take three years to formulate and might reshape human understanding of the cosmos are "deceptively simple."

Pendulum Cosmology described the universe as a bounded oscillation. Not the old Big Bang-and-done model, not the eternal inflation landscape, but a structured cycle: expansion and contraction between defined limits, the cosmos swinging like a pendulum between maximum and minimum configurations. The math was elegant. The observational data fit. VB had helped refine the framework himself — specifically the periodic crystal lattice structure that governed how the oscillation propagated through higher-dimensional configuration space. It was, by any measure, his most cited work. He was proud of it. He was also bored by it, because the interesting question had never been *how does the pendulum swing* but rather *what's on the other side of the swing*.

The configuration space was the key. In Pendulum Cosmology, the universe occupied a position within a vast mathematical landscape of possible configurations. As the pendulum oscillated, it traced a path through this space. But configuration space wasn't infinite — it was constrained by the boundary conditions of the oscillation itself. And those constraints meant something.

VB pulled up his sensitivity analysis. The part that had kept him up until 2 AM three nights running.

The boundary conditions of the oscillation defined a region in configuration space. Within that region, the physics was fixed — coupling constants, particle masses, the whole menu. Universe B, home sweet home. But the boundary was a boundary *of* something. On the other side of it — separated by what VB had started calling the "wiggle room," the narrow band of configuration space between conjugate sectors — was another region. Another set of boundary conditions. Another universe.

Universe A.

This wasn't new in principle. Pendulum Cosmology had always allowed for conjugate sectors. The math permitted it the way the math permitted a lot of things — unicorns were permitted by the math if you were creative enough with your boundary conditions. The question was whether the wiggle room between sectors was wide or narrow. Wide meant the conjugate sector could be anything — different coupling constants, different particle content, a universe where electrons had the mass of bowling balls and nothing interesting ever formed. Narrow meant the conjugate sector was constrained to be *similar*. Not identical. But close.

VB's hands moved across the keyboard — the fingertips of his left hand slightly rougher than the right, the calluses there so old he'd stopped noticing them. He had the tensor decomposition up on the center screen — the oscillation Hamiltonian expressed in the full configuration-space basis. Last week, he'd noticed something. The cross-coupling terms between sectors — the mathematical connections between Universe B's configuration and whatever lay on the other side of the boundary — were small. Not zero. Small.

That was the first domino.

He ran his ring finger slowly along the edge of the desk without noticing — back and forth, back and forth, the laminate there worn glassy-smooth from months of the same motion. It was what his hands did when his brain was fully somewhere else. He didn't notice it. He never noticed it.

If the cross-terms were small, the Hamiltonian wanted to factorize. It wanted to decompose into independent sectors, each governed by its own internal dynamics. And if it factorized cleanly, each sector would be free to have wildly different physics. But it didn't factorize cleanly. The cross-terms were small but nonzero, and that residual coupling imposed a constraint — the coupling delta, a single parameter that measured how tightly the boundary conditions of one sector were bound to the other.

VB had spent two weeks characterizing that constraint. The residual coupling between sectors forced a near-isomorphism. Not an exact mirror. A bounded deviation. The configurations on the other side of the wiggle room had to be *close* to the configurations on this side, with the deviation bounded by delta as a function of the coupling strength.

He'd calculated delta. It was small. Absurdly, terrifyingly, *wonderfully* small.

"Italy," he said, not looking up from the screen, "if delta is this small, then the conjugate sector doesn't just have similar physics. It has similar *structures*. Similar chemistry. Similar..." He trailed off, fingers hovering over the keyboard.

Similar planets. Similar biology. Similar people.

Not identical. The math didn't permit identical — the coupling ensured deviation, the way a photocopy always drifts from the original. The universe compressed its own possibilities into a narrow band and then reconstructed them on the other side of the boundary, and the reconstruction was close, bounded, constrained to resemble the source — but never perfect. There would be differences. Small ones. The kind that accumulated like translation errors across a long chain of copies. An almost-Earth. An almost-everything.

He sat back in his chair. The building hummed at 14 Hz — the resonant frequency of the HVAC system, which he'd identified his first week at the Briar and which now functioned as a kind of tectonic lullaby. The hum was always there. You stopped hearing it after a month. VB had never stopped hearing it. He liked it. It meant the building was working.

"If this is right," he said to the ceiling, "then there's another Earth. Close enough to recognize. Different enough to matter."

Italy remained noncommittal.

VB pulled up a fresh buffer and started running a convergence check. This was the part where he found out whether he was a genius or an idiot, and experience suggested the latter was more common but the former was more fun. The key was the sensitivity analysis — how robust was the small-delta result to perturbations in the boundary conditions? If you nudged the oscillation parameters by a reasonable amount, did delta stay small, or did it blow up and take the near-isomorphism with it?

He set up the parameter sweep. Twelve boundary condition parameters, each varied across a physically motivated range. The computation would take about forty minutes on his partition of the Briar's compute cluster. He submitted the job, took a sip of the terrible coffee, and leaned back.

He lasted about ninety seconds.

The Briar had a small kitchen on each residential floor. VB kept a shelf stocked — olive oil, parmesan, spaghetti — maintained the way he maintained everything he relied on, quietly, replacing things before they ran out. He filled a pot and salted the water properly. Not the pinch. The amount that made pasta taste like pasta.

He stood and waited for it to boil. There was nothing to optimize about this. Water boiled when it boiled. He found this restful — the suspension of the problem until a physical process completed at its own pace, the pace the chemistry required and not the pace he preferred.

Nine minutes. He drained it, returned it to the pot with olive oil and a tin of sardines, parmesan grated directly over the top. Carried the bowl back to his office.

Thirty-one minutes to go. He ate and watched the progress bar and didn't taste a single bite. His left hand, resting on the desk beside the bowl, tapped a slow pattern against the laminate — four beats, descending, a pause, four beats again. He didn't notice he was doing it. He never noticed.

---

The sky was doing what the sky always did now, which was the most beautiful thing any human had ever seen.

Tiny flashes peppered the darkness. Not stars — you could still see stars behind them, through them — but something layered in front, like the atmosphere itself was carbonated. Each flash was a Drift Node absorbing a blazar photon: a brief flare of captured energy, blue-white and instantaneous, gone before you could focus on it but replaced by another and another and another, thousands per second across the visible sky. The effect was champagne. The effect was phosphorescence. The effect was a canopy of light that shimmered and popped and sparkled in a way that made every sky that had come before it look static and dead by comparison.

People had gotten married under the carbonated sky. Children born after the EAF went online had never known a sky that didn't sparkle. To them, old photos of a plain dark sky looked broken.

VB watched it for maybe five seconds on the right monitor. It was beautiful. It was always beautiful. It was also the largest symptom of the largest problem in human history, and he had been living with that knowledge for long enough that the beauty had curdled into something more complicated. Not ugly. Never ugly. But heavy. Like watching someone you love smile at a party while you know the test results they don't.

The EAF worked. That was the thing. It *worked*. Every Drift Node in the orbital shells was doing exactly what it was designed to do — absorbing the electromagnetic radiation from TXS 0506+056, the blazar that had been aimed at Earth like a cosmic fire hose for fifteen years. The radiation was captured. The energy was harvested. The engineering was, by any historical standard, a triumph. VB had helped design the field architecture himself, back when the word "neutrino" didn't make his stomach clench.

Because the neutrinos were the real problem. They had always been the real problem.

He minimized the sky feed and pulled up the latest biological correlation report from the Briar's epidemiology group. He didn't need to — he'd memorized the key figures — but there was a difference between knowing a number and looking at it, the way there was a difference between knowing someone was dead and seeing the body.

Fifteen years of high-energy neutrino flux. Fifteen years of particles that passed through the EAF like it wasn't there, through the orbital shells, through the atmosphere, through buildings and bedrock and people, depositing tiny amounts of energy with each weak-force interaction, scrambling molecular bonds at random, degrading DNA repair mechanisms one hit at a time.

Years one through five: nothing. Background noise. The public celebrated the EAF. Politicians gave speeches.

Years five through eight: the signal emerged. Cancer rates ticked upward. Not dramatically. Not in headlines. Crop yields plateaued. Insect populations thinned.

Years eight through twelve: the pattern sharpened. The cancer curves steepened. Fertility clinic visits increased. A study out of Kyoto demonstrated a statistically significant correlation between neutrino flux density and developmental delays in children born after the blazar activation. The study was replicated in Lagos. In Sao Paulo. In Montreal.

VB closed the dataset. He didn't need to see the projection curves again. He'd helped build them. The biological damage function was going nonlinear. The system being damaged — the biosphere, the totality of living things on Earth — was reaching the part of the curve where repair mechanisms couldn't keep up with the accumulation rate. The word "collapse" had started appearing in internal reports. Not public ones. The public looked at the carbonated sky and felt safe because the sky was beautiful and the shield was working and someone, somewhere, was handling the rest.

The rest was neutrinos. The rest was VB's job.

He stared at the dataset's icon after closing it. The numbers sat in him like a stone in water — displacing something, pressing down, occupying a space that couldn't be used for anything else. Children in Kyoto. Developmental delays. He thought about that sometimes at 2 AM, the specific weight of it, and his chest would do something that had nothing to do with void anxiety and everything to do with the simple, crushing arithmetic of time running out for people who couldn't run.

Not the meeting. Not the math. This. This was what sat at the bottom of everything.

He pushed back from the desk. Walked to the wall. Pressed his palm flat against it.

---

Not because of the neutrino data. Not directly. The trigger had been subtler than that — a flash of the convergence plot on the center monitor, the twelve parameters sweeping through configuration space, and somewhere in the visual sweep his brain had snagged on the topology of it, the way the parameter space curved inward at the boundaries, and the inward curve had become a different inward curve, the one at the center of HTM, the mechanism that made the whole framework function, and then the room was too large and the darkness beyond his monitors was too deep and the space between objects was too much like the space inside a —

He pressed his palm flat against the wall — left hand, always the left, the callused fingertips catching slightly on the painted surface.

Solid. Dense. Real. The wall had mass and extent and a surface temperature of approximately 19 degrees Celsius and it was *there*, occupying space the way things were supposed to occupy space, and his hand was on it, and the building hummed at 14 Hz, and the floor held him and the ceiling held the floor above and the whole structure was a vast assembly of matter and engineering that was exactly as dense and exactly as volumetric as it should be.

Four times a week. Sometimes five. He'd never told anyone.

The worst part wasn't the panic. The worst part was the solitude of it — the specific loneliness of a man who built his career around the thing that made his hands shake, standing in a quiet office at 2 AM pressing his palm against a wall because there was no one to press it against instead. No one who knew this happened. No one who'd ever walked in and found him here, back straight, breathing through it, winning a fight nobody knew he was in.

He pressed his hand harder. Breathed. Let the 14 Hz hum carry him back to solid ground.

"Okay," he said quietly. "Okay. We're fine. We're good."

He went back to his desk. The convergence job had twelve minutes left. His coffee was lukewarm. Italy watched from the ceiling, shaped like a country that contained, as far as VB knew, zero singularities.

He sat down. He drank the lukewarm terrible coffee. He was fine.

---

His terminal chimed. The convergence job was done.

VB pulled up the results and felt his pulse do something his cardiovascular system probably hadn't intended. The parameter sweep — twelve boundary condition parameters, each varied across the full physically motivated range — showed delta remaining small. Not just small. Stable. The near-isomorphism held across the entire parameter space. You could nudge the oscillation parameters, stretch the boundary conditions, push the wiggle room in every direction the physics allowed, and the conjugate sector stayed close. Bounded. Constrained to resemble Universe B — the way a compressed signal, released and reconstructed, retains the architecture of the original even as it loses the details.

He stared at the convergence plot. Twelve panels, one per parameter, each showing delta as a function of the perturbation magnitude. Twelve flat lines. Twelve parameters that didn't matter because the topology of configuration space forced the result regardless.

"Italy," he whispered. "Italy, I think I have something."

He ran the numbers again. Not because he thought they were wrong — the computation was deterministic, it would give the same answer every time — but because running them again was what you did when the answer was this big. You double-checked. You triple-checked. You sat in your chair at 2 AM in a quiet building and you made sure the universe was really saying what you thought it was saying.

It was.

The wiggle room between conjugate sectors was narrow. Narrow enough that the other side wasn't just another set of physical laws — it was another version of *this*. The boundary conditions of the Pendulum oscillation compressed the configuration space so tightly that an Earth-like planet in the conjugate sector was not a coincidence to be hoped for but a near-inevitability to be expected. The deviation was bounded by the coupling delta. The structures were required to be similar. Similar but not identical — reconstructed through the boundary, carrying the architecture of the original with the imperfections of the passage. An almost-Earth. Real enough to recognize. Changed enough to grieve.

VB saved the results. Encrypted the file. Closed the directory. Reopened BUDGET PROJECTIONS FY27 and verified that the nested folders still looked boring enough to repel human curiosity.

He couldn't tell anyone. Not yet. Maybe not for months. The result was too explosive and the implications were too entangled with HTM and the mission. If he walked into the review board meeting and said "by the way, I can prove another Earth exists on the other side," they would either think he was insane or they would get so distracted by the philosophical implications that the actual agenda — the engineering, the monopoles, the neutrino solution — would get buried. He needed them focused on the mechanism. On the physics of getting there.

Wiggle Room could wait. Wiggle Room was the answer to a question nobody was asking yet.

He sat with that for a moment. The weight of it. Knowing something this large and having no one to tell. He was used to carrying things alone — the void anxiety, the mission math, the 2 AM walls — but this was different. This was joy trapped in a sealed container. The most extraordinary result of his career, and it would live in a folder called BUDGET PROJECTIONS, and he would walk into tomorrow's meeting and present the mechanism and argue the engineering and never mention that he already knew what they'd find on the other side, and the distance between what he knew and what he could say would sit in his chest like a held breath for months.

He finished the coffee. It had gone fully cold, which transformed it from "hot rust" to "cold rust," which was objectively worse but also somehow more honest. He set the mug down on the sticky note. Looked at the clock.

2:41 AM.

He should sleep. The meeting was at 9. He should absolutely, definitely, without question go to bed right now.

His eyes drifted to the door.

Down the hall, three turns and an elevator, was the signals lab. And in the signals lab was a terminal connected to the 55 Cancri e relay.

"No," he said out loud. "Bed. You're going to bed."

He stood up. Stretched. His back made a sound that was either a crack or a small protest.

He walked to the door. Opened it. Turned left.

Toward the signals lab.

"Damn it," he muttered, but he was already walking.

---

The Mimic Anomaly had been running for longer than VB had been at the Briar.

55 Cancri e was a super-Earth — roughly twice the diameter, eight times the mass, tidally locked in a hellish close orbit around its star. Surface temperatures on the day side hot enough to melt lead. By every conventional measure, a terrible place to look for anything interesting. But decades ago, someone had bounced modulated signals off the planet's crust — structured electromagnetic pulses — and the signals had come back structured. Not random reflections. Shaped responses. Same shape as the input. Every time. Without exception.

The scientific consensus: natural resonance. The lithium-rich crust acting as a piezoelectric medium, converting electromagnetic input into mechanical oscillation and back, preserving signal structure the way a canyon preserved an echo. Remarkable geology. Not life. The Mimic Anomaly — that was the official designation now — had been studied, published on, conferenced about, and eventually filed under *interesting but explained* in the catalogue of cosmic curiosities.

Then somebody decided the problem was lag. If the Mimic was just a mirror, fine — but what if something *else* was out there, watching, waiting for a properly formed signal, and the round-trip time was the obstacle? What if the mirror was a door and they just hadn't knocked fast enough?

So they built the relay. They spent monopoles they couldn't spare — irreplaceable, asteroid-sourced, desperately needed elsewhere — on a compressed-spacetime thread between Earth and 55 Cancri e. The relay made the round-trip instantaneous. Or close enough.

Same mirror. Instant now.

VB had read the relay proposal once and sat with the specific feeling of watching very smart people solve the wrong problem at enormous cost. He understood the impulse. When you were out of other options, *faster* felt like progress. He came here anyway, most nights, because he was the kind of person who couldn't leave a locked door alone even after he'd accepted it wasn't going to open.

The signals lab was dark. He flipped on the overheads — 4000 Kelvin, cooler than his office, which always made the room feel like it was slightly disappointed in him — and dropped into the chair in front of the relay terminal. He swiveled it slightly, automatically, so the window was behind him and the room was in front. Force of habit. He worked better facing inward. He'd never examined why.

The system was live. It was always live.

VB typed.

`HELLO`

He waited. The turnaround was near-instantaneous — the relay network handled the transit time, buffered and corrected.

`HELLO`

Standard. Expected. The mirror held up its end. HELLO gets HELLO. The universe echoes.

`WHO ARE YOU`

A pause. Shorter than it should have been.

`WHO ARE YOU`

Still mirroring. The Mimic doing what the Mimic always did. VB rested his fingers on the keyboard. The lab was silent except for the hum of the cooling system and the faint ubiquitous 14 Hz.

He knew what would come back. He always knew what would come back. That was the entire point of the Mimic Anomaly — it returned exactly what you gave it, faithfully, without addition or subtraction. You could sit at this terminal for the rest of your life and never get a word you hadn't sent first. He knew this. He was here anyway.

It was late. He was tired. The convergence results were still glowing in his mind — the flat lines of delta across twelve parameters, the near-isomorphism, the narrow wiggle room, the other Earth that the math said was practically required to exist. A universe that compressed and reconstructed reality, bounded and constrained, never perfectly.

He wasn't thinking about what he typed next. It just came out.

`ALONE`

The cursor blinked. The response appeared.

`ALONE`

VB stared at the screen.

He'd known it was coming. He'd known before his fingers hit the keys. The Mimic returned what you gave it — that was all it had ever done, all it would ever do, the one guaranteed and unbroken fact about this terminal. He had known.

And it still landed.

The mirror had done what the mirror always did. Returned exactly what he'd given it, faithfully, precisely, without addition or subtraction. A perfect echo. And the echo contained a word he hadn't meant to say, or rather a word he'd meant but hadn't meant to *transmit*, the way you sometimes say something to an empty room because the room is empty and then the room says it back and suddenly the room isn't empty anymore, it's full of the thing you said, and the thing you said was *true*. He'd known the Mimic would send it back. He'd sent it anyway. That was the part he didn't have a clean explanation for.

The word hung on the screen like an accusation. Or a diagnosis. Four letters, sent forty light-years, and it came back unchanged because of course it did — the universe was a system that returned your inputs, compressed through distance and reconstructed on arrival, structurally intact but stripped of everything except the bare content. No comfort added. No context. Just the word, sitting there, true.

He sat with it. The fluorescent lights hummed. The terminal cursor blinked.

2:58 AM. Alone in a lab in the largest research facility on Earth, surrounded by two thousand sleeping colleagues and a planet of seven billion people and an orbital shell of thousands of Drift Nodes catching photons from a star that was trying to kill all of them, and the word on the screen was ALONE and it had come from his own fingers and the planet had sent it back like a receipt.

He let the moment exist. He didn't perform for it or narrate it or deflect it. He just sat.

Then the moment passed, the way moments do when you're the kind of person who can't leave anything alone.

He typed, hard, with the focused aggression of a man who wanted a different answer:

`BUTTHOLE`

`BUTTHOLE`

"Oh, come *on*," he said to the screen. Not laughing. Genuinely frustrated, the way you get frustrated with a door that won't open or a universe that won't tell you anything you don't already know. "Say something else. Say *anything* else. One word. One word that isn't mine."

The cursor blinked. 55 Cancri e, forty light-years away, said nothing new.

VB pushed back from the terminal. Rubbed his face with both hands.

"Augustus?"

He spun the chair around. Dr. Lena Chowdhury, atmospheric dynamics, standing in the doorway with a tablet under one arm and the expression of someone who had expected an empty lab and gotten a grown man arguing with a planet.

"Everything okay?" she asked.

The mask was on before the chair stopped spinning. Casual. Light. The VB that everyone at the Briar knew and liked, the quick one, the funny one, the one who made you feel like every interaction was the most entertaining part of his day. It cost him something, that switch. It always cost him something, and he always paid it, and nobody ever saw the transaction.

"Oh, yeah, totally," he said, gesturing vaguely at the terminal. "Just messing with the alien communicator thing. Seeing if it wants to chat."

Lena smiled. The polite smile of a colleague who had somewhere to be at 3 AM. "And? Any luck?"

"It called me a butthole, so. Relationship's progressing."

"Sounds about right." She shifted the tablet to her other arm. "Well, Dr. Veil, get some rest. HTM meeting tomorrow. If you can really convince them to send someone to retrieve monopoles from another universe, you're gonna need to be at a hundred percent."

There it was. The meeting. The thing he'd been not thinking about all night, the way you don't think about an exam by studying everything except looking at the date.

"Yeah, you right," he said. Rubbed the back of his head. "Headed to bed now."

"Good night, Augustus."

"Night, Lena."

She walked off down the corridor, her footsteps fading into the 14 Hz hum. VB sat in the chair. The mask came off. The room was empty again, and the word ALONE was still on the screen behind him, and Lena had been standing right there and hadn't noticed a thing, which was exactly how it was supposed to work and exactly why it worked and exactly the problem.

He closed the terminal. Killed the overheads. Walked out.

---

His quarters were on the residential level, two floors up. Small, functional. A bed, a desk, a window he kept curtained because the carbonated sky meant something he didn't want to sleep next to. A shelf of books. A photo of his parents. A small cactus that Simon had given him as a joke about VB's ability to keep things alive, which VB had kept alive out of pure spite for three years running.

He brushed his teeth. Set his alarm for 7:30. Sat on the edge of the bed.

Tomorrow he would stand in front of the review board. Sixty-four people. Engineers, physicists, mission architects, at least two who'd published direct critiques of HTM. He would present the Horizon Transfer Model — the mechanism for encoding information, transiting an event horizon, and reconstructing it on the other side. He would present the monopole case — why magnetic monopoles were the only known particle capable of generating a field that could scatter neutrinos at sufficient scale. He would present the conjugate sector as the only known location where monopoles were predicted by the lattice geometry. He would present the mission architecture.

And they would push back. Simon was right — they would push back on reconstruction fidelity, on the information encoding scheme, on whether the theoretical framework was mature enough to risk a human life. They would push back because that was their job and because the proposal was, by any reasonable standard, insane. Send a person through a black hole into another universe to get particles that might save the world. It sounded like something a desperate civilization would try because they'd run out of better ideas.

It was something a desperate civilization would try because they'd run out of better ideas.

VB lay back on the bed. Stared at the ceiling. This ceiling had no water stain, no Italy, no familiar geography to anchor him. Just flat white panels and a smoke detector with a blinking green LED.

He thought about the neutrino data. The biological damage curve going nonlinear. The children. The developmental delays. The soil. The insects. The slow, invisible, polite apocalypse that was happening while the sky sparkled and the world congratulated itself on solving the problem.

He thought about Wiggle Room. The flat convergence lines. Delta staying small. The other Earth — compressed and reconstructed through the boundary conditions of the Pendulum oscillation, carrying the architecture of this universe with the imperfections of the passage. Close but not the same. Never the same.

He thought about ALONE, glowing on a terminal screen, sent forty light-years and returned unchanged.

He closed his eyes. The building hummed at 14 Hz. The alarm was set. The meeting was in six hours. The math was right. The mission was necessary. The sky was beautiful and the world was dying and tomorrow Augustus Veil was going to walk into a room and ask humanity to do the most impossible thing it had ever attempted, and he was going to do it on six hours of sleep and terrible coffee and the unshakable, irrational, probably-arrogant conviction that he was the right person to ask.

He was asleep in four minutes.

The sky carbonated above the Briar. The neutrinos passed through everything. And in an encrypted folder labeled BUDGET PROJECTIONS FY27, twelve flat lines held steady across every perturbation, pointing toward an Earth that no one in this universe knew about yet — compressed from the same source, reconstructed through the same constraints, close enough to break your heart.
